“Magic One” Cube Design Project—Intro 01.31.25

W. jeff cunningham

You find me.

This project is towards creating a D&D-style manual but oriented at Magic’s player base. It would contain a recommended card-list, recommended versions, recommended formats, lore, and original art by old school Magic artists (ideally). In other words, to create a prestige cube manual (or cube manual series), well-made enough to stand above amateur designs.

In a way, it’s an attempt to return Magic to its fundamentals–polishing it off so it’s new again—in contrast to the contemporary text-heavy Arena and branding-oriented designs.

Key selling points:

  • Optimized to facilitate on-boarding new and more casual gamers—girlfriends, moms, buddies, etc—while maintaining strategic depth for experts. Low cognitive “download”—cards are simple, elegant, easy to understand.

What are you at?

  • One-time-buy. Although the cube could be designed to be modular (e.g. 540 card ‘base set’ that you can add more ‘sets’ onto), but you can use your own cards if you prefer, and the set is designed to have tons of replayability right out of the box. Great basic draft format; a variety of other formats for any number of players; some kind of constructed format(s). Perfect for just parking at the family cabin or the game shelf.
  • Variety of formats, again—adaptable to different settings. Good for deep play, tinkering, running some games at the bar, draft night, multiplayer with the fam, etc.
  • Budget! We’d be price conscious and although we might recommend some more expensive cards for those who are willing, we’d be aiming for a lower price point (<$1,000) for people traditionally priced out of the cube market. As well, of course, we can recommend a range of versions for someone who may want to put together a cheaper list. And if you already own the cards, go for it; even join our constructed league, it’s free.
  • Good investment. As part of the design, we could highlight cards that have value along previously overlooked vectors, such as elegance as a game piece, templating, literary flavor text (Religious texts; Shakespeare; Romantic Poets, etc), first printings, and art. Some reserved list cards or other under printed cards are worth pennies, simply because no one has found a way to use them. These cards can spike easily.

  • Doesn’t feel commercial. This is a cube that a teacher could show their students and have their parents be impressed, countering the image of TCGs as money pits and aesthetic cesspools.
  • Interesting content and nostalgia. As a deeply enfranchised player myself, speculation on the best “remix” or fundamental design tenets is fascinating. “A new interpretation of Richard Garfield’s classic design” would be interesting reading for its own sake. In the vein of “the ReSleevables.”

As well, of course, there are possibilities to team with a retailer, and launch other content or OP of some kind. I think these particulars along with a GoFundMe campaign could be worked out after the core concept is more refined.

“So easy a baby could play.” How cool would a YouTube video be of two 4-year-olds who don’t know how to read yet, slugging it out with Grey Ogres and Hill Giants? Counter your Giant Growth, bruh. Fireball, dome.

I think the full art cards are great; absolute beginners could order the recommended text versions, but intermediate to advanced players might prefer the splashier versions.

The intention of this document is to get some basic design notes down towards initial playtesting sessions.

Broadly, I’ve been imagining this project as starting from Alpha and extending to present times, with some bias for nostalgia (and reserved list cards), filtering for clean, consistent templating, and easily grokkable cards. So you dump the Balduvian Shamans, the 3rd Edition Control Magics, the outmoded abilities or rules text, and keep whatever’s left.

Which is most appealing?

Here’s a rough list of potentials, up to about 2010: https://cubecobra.com/cube/overview/153e8760-b668-422b-8fde-2cde1b837714

Lots of sweet cards from later on, but I don’t know them as well.

The key task at this point is designing a fundamentally appealing and FUN “base set.” It could be skewed towards the earlier stuff (which would be ideal for the hedge fund aspect), or include more hits from across the board.

  1. Working out a card list. The primary format has to be booster draft.
  2. Working out a variety of formats, tailored for different purposes and skill levels.
  3. Aesthetic considerations. What should the set look like? Black borders vs white borders?

 

(1)—Cutting the 3500+ cards that might be suitable into a 360 or 540 card list.

One big takeaway from the Christmas draft is that the elegant but underpowered game pieces, while looking cool, do bias impressions, and have some negative gameplay implications.

Beautiful cards! Playable, rarely. Accommodating them requires a lot of warping.

What I find really cool about some of these old underpowered cards is that they represent a way to play and understand the original game that is usually obscured (kind of like lost code in old video games. “What were they trying to do?” “What did we miss?”). Color status was a huge theme in early MTG, but you can never really “feel” it as a Magic player, because the cards are so underpowered. By setting the power level lower, these cards can come into focus. This was the idea behind my last year’s underpowered “jank binder” cube, trying to bring out new play patterns from old cards.

HOWEVER—I’ve come to recognize there is a meaningful correlation between power level and “fun.”

Besides the optics, one critical point is that powerful cards tend to be cheaper and less situational, which means that on any given turn you have more options, which gives you more agency over the outcome of the game.

So the elegant but underpowered cards need to be used much more sparingly.

(I’m also on the fence about the Legend rule we played at Christmas, especially with the janky Legends legends. Maybe this could be an “Expansion kit”—100 recommended Legends, guildhouse, etc and the rules for the new variant.)

The old cards can be basic but should be contoured to exist within a powerful and fun format. This in itself is another cool aesthetic effect, because it makes the oldest cards feel new and fresh again.

(2)—Within a larger cube, one could create several mini-cubes: a DC-10 cube, a Battlebox cube, Jumpstart cube, Precon decks, etc. As well, one could also create a variety of formats—a multiplayer variant, a commander variant, etc.

Think of, e.g., mini-shogi variants.

Maybe they could also match a skill progression: a 16-card ‘cube’ for absolute beginners, a 32-card “mana dice”/Hearthstone variant, a 64-card Dominion variant that introduces more abilities, a 128-card DC-10 draft deck (kind of like Exploding Kittens), a 256-card battlebox, etc. I’m not sure exactly what these should be or how they should look yet.

I would also love to work out a League format that might involve trading, attempting to simulate the early or pre-Alpha days of MTG.

You could also even do silly stuff like include a Tarot deck in there.

So these need to be developed as well.

(3)—Aesthetic considerations. What should the set look like? Black borders vs white borders?

Briefly, there needs to be some consideration of the overall “look”, which exists in tension with the priority on best templating.
